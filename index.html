How Pregnancy Due Date Calculation Works

The most common method for estimating due dates is Naegele's Rule, which adds 280 days (40 weeks) to the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP). This method assumes a regular 28-day cycle and is widely used by healthcare professionals.

While due dates are estimates (only about 5% of babies arrive exactly on their due date), they provide an important timeline for tracking pregnancy progress and planning for your baby's arrival.

Understanding Pregnancy Weeks and Trimesters

Pregnancy is typically divided into three trimesters, each with distinct developmental milestones:

First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)

The foundation period where major organs begin forming. Many women experience morning sickness, fatigue, and other early pregnancy symptoms during this time.

Second Trimester (Weeks 13-26)

Often called the "honeymoon period" of pregnancy. Morning sickness typically subsides, energy returns, and many women feel their baby's first movements.

Third Trimester (Weeks 27-40+)

The final stretch where the baby gains weight rapidly and prepares for birth. Mothers may experience increased discomfort as they approach their due date.

Important Considerations

While online due date calculators provide helpful estimates, remember:

  • Consult your healthcare provider for medical advice and official due date confirmation
  • Ultrasound measurements in early pregnancy often provide the most accurate due date
  • Only about 5% of babies are born exactly on their estimated due date
  • If you have irregular periods, the LMP method may be less accurate

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is the due date calculator?

Our calculator uses the standard medical formula (Naegele's Rule) and provides estimates comparable to those used by healthcare providers. However, ultrasound confirmation in early pregnancy typically provides the most accurate due date.

What if I don't know my LMP?

If you're unsure of your last menstrual period date, your healthcare provider can estimate your due date using ultrasound measurements, which are especially accurate in the first trimester.

Can due dates change?

Yes, healthcare providers may adjust your due date based on ultrasound measurements, especially if there's a significant difference from the LMP-based calculation or if you have irregular periods.
